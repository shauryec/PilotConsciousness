01

Why Now

The FAA's Part 5 rule requires every Part 135 charter and commuter operator — not just the large ones — to develop, implement, and declare a compliant Safety Management System. Implementation plans were due in late 2024. The full declaration of compliance is due 28 May 2027. Operators who wait until the deadline is close are already behind; a functioning SMS has to be demonstrated in operation, not assembled the month before.

Most operators this size have never had to run one, and don't have the headcount to build a safety department from scratch just to satisfy a filing. They have an operations manual, a chief pilot who is also flying the line, and now a federal requirement to prove — with evidence, not paperwork — that someone is reading the safety record and acting on it.

A template manual satisfies the letter of that requirement and misses the point of it entirely. An SMS nobody reads is a dial with nothing behind it — the audit passed, the binder complete, and no one who would notice the beat lengthening. We exist to be the ones who notice — and to carry the operational weight of the system so you don't have to build a department to meet a deadline.

What Stays. What Moves.

The smallest internal footprint the regulation allows, and nothing smaller than that.

Stays with you

What the FAA won't let leave

The certificate holder's signatory authority, and an Accountable Executive with real, demonstrable control over the operation. These cannot be delegated to an outside firm under any arrangement, and we don't pretend otherwise.

Runs through us

Everything an SMS actually requires

  • Safety Risk Management — hazard identification, risk assessment
  • Safety Assurance — audits, internal evaluation, corrective action tracking
  • Safety Promotion — training content, communication, culture work
  • Hazard reporting intake, records, and the Declaration of Compliance package
  • Reading the record — continuously, not once a quarter

Exactly which roles are legally irreducible depends on your certificate's size and structure. We confirm the precise split with you and your counsel before anything is signed — the principle above is the target, not a guess.

How It Works

Decentralized ownership. Centralized operation.

I

We build the system

Not a template. An SMS structured around your actual operation — your aircraft, your routes, your existing records — because that's what the regulation actually requires and what a boilerplate manual only pretends to satisfy.

II

We run it

Day to day, not just at audit time. Reports come to us. We administer the hazard register, the risk assessments, the training cadence, the recordkeeping — the operational labor of an SMS, carried by us instead of built out in-house.

III

We read across every operator we run

The part a single in-house hire cannot do. A pattern that looks like an isolated fluke inside one operation is often the third time we've seen it this quarter, at a different operator, on the same equipment or route structure. That comparison only exists because we're reading more than one operation's record.

IV

You sign what's true

Your Accountable Executive reviews and signs; we've already done the reading. The declaration of compliance reflects a system that has actually been run, not assembled the week before it was due.
