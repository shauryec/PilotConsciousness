CONSCIENCE/An excerpt

Movement II

The Drift

People are dying at the exact coordinates where conscience used to be.

I want to be careful with that sentence, because it sounds like rhetoric and it isn't. It's a finding. If we take the accident reports — the real ones, the long ones, the ones with the timelines reconstructed second by second — and we read past the proximate cause, we find the same shape again and again. Someone knew. Someone in the room, or on the frequency, or at the console, registered that something was wrong. And then they did not act on what they registered.

There is always a moment. Forty seconds, sometimes ninety, sometimes four unhurried minutes, in which someone knew. Not suspected — knew. The first officer watching the airspeed bleed away. The operator watching the pressure climb past where pressure climbs. The controller with the conflict already painted on the scope. The body registers it before the mind finds words; the document is written before there is language to read it back. This is wrong.

And then the most ordinary thing in the world happens. Nothing.

We say they when we read these reports — they failed, they didn't speak, they deferred — but the arithmetic that produced the silence is ours, and we run it every day without noticing we are running it. To speak is to claim one knows better than the procedure, better than the captain, better than the institution that certified them both. To speak is to become the difficult one. To speak and be wrong is to carry it for years. So the wrongness gets rounded down. The gap is called within tolerance.

One waits. One defers. One reads the silence of the others as evidence, when it is only the same arithmetic running in every head in the room at once.

And in the seconds it takes to defer, the aircraft descends through the last altitude at which the deferral could still have been undone. People die in that interval. Not because no one knew. Because everyone who knew was running the same calculation, and the calculation said wait.

We have a whole literature for this. We call it complacency, or normalization of deviance, or poor crew resource management, or a weak safety culture. These are good phrases. They are also, every one of them, a way of describing the hole without naming what fell into it.

What fell into it was conscience.

This is the opening of the second of four movements. The book goes on to ask the only question that matters after this one: not who to blame, but how the instrument gets picked back up.
