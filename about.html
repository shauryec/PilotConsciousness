The museum wing

A pilot, before anything else.

Everything in this work is built from instrument time — real hours in real airplanes, and the years afterward spent asking why the safest systems ever built still leave a gap exactly where a person's judgment should be.

Shaurye Chakravarty

Shaurye is a commercial pilot and flight instructor based in Anthem, in North Phoenix, Arizona. He holds commercial privileges in single- and multi-engine airplanes, an instrument rating, and instructor certificates that qualify him to teach primary students, instrument students, and multi-engine pilots alike.

He is currently a Master's candidate in Safety Science at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University, Prescott, where the Safety-II framework — the study of why systems succeed, not only why they fail — became the scaffolding for the argument in CONSCIENCE. He previously served as an Instructor Pilot at ERAU Prescott, and before aviation, trained as a filmmaker, earning a B.Sc. in Filmmaking between Whistling Woods International and Bharathidasan University.

That earlier life is not incidental. A filmmaker's eye for structure, image, and rhythm is exactly what the book uses to turn horology — the mechanics of clocks — into a working philosophy of conscience.

Beyond the manuscript, Shaurye's direction is simple to state and hard to overstate: conversations that produce change, and teaching people to think, without the chronic low-grade stress that safety-critical work usually asks a person to carry silently.

Exhibit: the substrate

What the book is built on top of

CONSCIENCE argues that every mind needs a restoring force — something amoral, all-inclusive, and verifiable, that a person can run and check against a life rather than simply believe. Shaurye's own substrate is the teaching of Nichiren, a thirteenth-century Japanese Buddhist monk, chosen because it describes cause and effect rather than issuing commands, and because it stakes its truth on actual, observable proof rather than doctrine.

The book doesn't ask anyone to adopt that substrate. It asks every reader to find their own — because a balance wheel without a hairspring doesn't run free. It just stops returning.
